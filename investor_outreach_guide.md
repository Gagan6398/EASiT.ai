## 1. Fundraising Strategy Overview

### The Seed Round Playbook for Easit.ai

**Target raise:** $2,000,000
**Instrument:** Priced round (Seed) or SAFE (Simple Agreement for Future Equity)
**Recommended structure:** SAFE notes at $8M-$12M post-money valuation cap (adjust based on traction and market conditions)

### Core Fundraising Principles

1. **Run a parallel process.** Contact 30-50 investors within a 2-3 week window. Never rely on a single investor.
2. **Create urgency through momentum.** Investors follow other investors. Once you get one term sheet, communicate momentum (without bluffing).
3. **Optimize for the right lead investor.** A strong lead (brand-name fund or respected angel) de-risks the round for followers.
4. **Fundraising is a full-time job.** Dedicate 60-80% of founder time during active fundraising. Sprint, don't marathon.
5. **Keep building while raising.** Every week of traction growth strengthens your hand.

### Funding Landscape Context (2025-2026)

- AI seed rounds are competitive but well-funded. Global AI VC investment exceeded $100B in 2024.
- Investors are increasingly focused on **differentiated AI** — not just wrappers on GPT/Claude. Easit.ai's hallucination verification engine is a genuine differentiator.
- Voice-first AI is a growing category. The conversational AI market is projected to reach $32B by 2028.
- **Trust and reliability** in AI is becoming the #1 enterprise concern — perfectly aligned with Easit.ai's thesis.

## 4. Cold Email Best Practices

### The Anatomy of a High-Converting Investor Email

```
SUBJECT LINE (under 50 chars, specific)
├── Personalized opening (1-2 sentences referencing their work)
├── What you're building (1 sentence)
├── The problem you solve (1 sentence with data)
├── Why it's working (1-2 traction proof points)
├── The ask (specific meeting time)
└── SIGNATURE (name, title, links)
```

### Rules for Easit.ai Cold Emails

1. **Lead with the hallucination problem.** It's timely, investors experience it themselves, and it's a $B+ problem. Every investor has been frustrated by ChatGPT making things up.

2. **Never attach a deck in the first email.** Link to your live product instead: `https://easit-ai-less-pzzi.vercel.app/`

3. **Show, don't tell.** Instead of "we have great technology," say "our verification engine has grounded 50,000+ claims with 99.2% accuracy."

4. **One email = one CTA.** Ask for a meeting. Don't ask them to review a deck AND schedule a call AND watch a video.

5. **Keep it under 150 words.** Investors receive 50-100 cold emails per week. Brevity is respect.

6. **Send Tuesday through Thursday, 8-10 AM** in the investor's timezone. Avoid Mondays (inbox overload) and Fridays (weekend mode).

7. **Use a real email address** (founder@easit.ai), not Gmail/Yahoo. Set up proper SPF/DKIM records.

### Subject Lines That Work for AI Startups

✅ **Good:**
- "Hallucination-free AI — live product + $2M seed"
- "Solving AI's trust problem with real-time grounding"
- "[FUND_NAME] + AI reliability — quick question"

❌ **Bad:**
- "Exciting AI startup looking for investment!!"
- "The next big thing in AI"
- "Easit.ai Series Seed Fundraise Opportunity"

### Channels by Investor Type

| Channel | Best For | Response Rate |
|---|---|---|
| **Cold email** | VCs, institutional investors | 10-20% |
| **Warm intro** | All (especially Tier 1) | 40-60% |

| **LinkedIn** | India VCs, enterprise investors | 10-15% |

## 5. Follow-Up Strategy

### The Follow-Up Cadence

```
Day 0:    Initial outreach (cold email or warm intro)
Day 5-7:  Follow-up #1 (add new info/traction update)
Day 14:   Follow-up #2 (share a relevant insight or demo video)
Day 21:   Follow-up #3 (final attempt — mention momentum if any)
Day 30+:  Move to "nurture" list — monthly update email
```

### Follow-Up Rules

1. **Always add new value.** Never send "just checking in" or "bumping this up." Each follow-up should contain new information:
   - New traction milestone
   - Relevant industry news that validates your thesis
   - A short demo video (60-90 seconds)
   - A quote from a beta user
   - News about another investor's interest (if appropriate)

2. **Reply to your original thread.** Don't start a new email chain.

3. **3 follow-ups maximum.** After 3 unanswered follow-ups, move to the nurture list. Don't burn bridges.

4. **If they say "not right now":**
   - Ask: "Would it be helpful if I sent you a monthly update?"
   - Most will say yes. This keeps the door open for future rounds.

### Monthly Investor Update Template

Once you have investors on your nurture list, send a monthly update:

## 6. Handling Rejections & Passes

### Common Rejection Reasons & Responses

| Rejection | What It Means | How to Respond |
|---|---|---|
| "Too early for us" | They want more traction | "Totally understand. Can I add you to our monthly update? We're hitting milestones fast." |
| "Not in our thesis" | Genuine misalignment | "Appreciate the transparency. Is there another partner at [FUND] who focuses on AI applications?" |
| "We need to see revenue" | They want proof of monetization | "We're launching our enterprise API next quarter. Can I circle back when we have paying customers?" |
| "Market is too crowded" | They don't see differentiation | "I'd love to show you our hallucination verification engine — it's fundamentally different from wrapper products. 5-minute demo?" |
| "We're full on AI deals" | Portfolio conflict or saturation | "Got it. Is there a later-stage fund you'd recommend that's actively looking at AI reliability?" |
| No response (ghosted) | Low priority / inbox overload | Follow up 2 more times, then move to nurture list |

### Rejection Mindset

- **80% of investors will say no.** This is normal. Even the best startups get rejected by most investors.
- **A "no" today can become a "yes" next round.** Keep relationships warm.
- **Ask for feedback.** "What would you need to see to get to a yes?" is the most valuable question you can ask.
- **Ask for referrals.** "Is there another investor you think would be a better fit?" converts rejections into warm intros.
- **Never argue with a pass.** Thank them, ask for feedback, and move on.

## 11. Negotiation & Term Sheet Basics

### Key Terms to Understand

| Term | What It Means | Typical Seed Range |
|---|---|---|
| **Pre-money valuation** | Company value before investment | $6M-$15M for AI seed |
| **Post-money valuation** | Pre-money + investment amount | $8M-$17M |
| **SAFE vs. Priced Round** | SAFE is simpler; priced round has more terms | SAFEs common at seed |
| **Valuation Cap** | Maximum valuation for SAFE conversion | $8M-$12M recommended |
| **Discount** | Discount on next round price for SAFE | 15-25% typical |
| **Pro-rata rights** | Investor's right to invest in future rounds | Standard for lead |
| **Board seat** | Investor seat on your board | Not typical at seed |
| **Information rights** | Quarterly/annual reporting obligations | Standard |

### Negotiation Tips

1. **Never share your target valuation first.** Let investors make offers. "We're talking to several firms and want to find the right partner and fair terms."

2. **Optimize for the right investor, not the highest valuation.** A $10M cap with a16z is worth more than a $15M cap with an unknown fund.

3. **Get everything in writing.** Verbal commitments are not binding. Push for signed term sheets.

4. **Use a startup lawyer.** Firms like Cooley, Wilson Sonsini, Gunderson Dettmer, and Orrick specialize in seed rounds. Some offer deferred billing.

5. **Standard SAFE (YC format) is your friend.** Don't overcomplicate. Post-money SAFE with a valuation cap is the simplest path.
